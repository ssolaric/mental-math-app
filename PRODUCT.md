# Product

## Register

product

## Users

5th graders, roughly 10–11 years old. They open the app on a laptop or tablet at home or in a classroom for short, self-directed practice sessions — often a few minutes between other activities. They can already read fluently, are competitive with themselves, and have used phones and games enough to expect snappy, well-made interactions. They are NOT toddlers; designing down to them is the fastest way to lose them.

The job to be done: get reps in on arithmetic facts (add, subtract, multiply, divide) at three difficulty tiers, see whether they're getting faster and more accurate, and feel a little pull to come back tomorrow.

## Product Purpose

A focused mental-math drill that rewards speed and accuracy without bribing the user. Success looks like: a kid sits down, picks an operation and difficulty, answers a steady stream of problems, sees their streak and score move, and walks away knowing whether they improved. Progress is local-only (no account, no backend); the app is the workout, not the leaderboard.

It is not a curriculum, not a tutor, not a homework replacement. It is one well-made room in the house: the practice room.

## Brand Personality

**Bold, fast, satisfying.**

- **Bold**: opinionated visual choices, strong color commitments, large type, big targets. Not loud-for-loud's-sake — confident.
- **Fast**: input-to-feedback latency is the headline feature. Submit on Enter, next problem appears instantly, no modal interruptions, no celebratory delays the kid has to wait through.
- **Satisfying**: feedback has weight. A correct answer should feel like a clean click; a streak should feel like it's building toward something. Reward is honest — tied to actual difficulty and speed, never engineered to hook.

Voice: direct, second-person, low word count. "Nice — 7 in a row." Not "Wow, amazing job superstar!!"

## Anti-references

This app should not look or feel like any of these:

- **Generic SaaS dashboard**: no navy/blue gradient backgrounds, no hero-metric template (big number + tiny label + supporting stats + gradient accent), no "MRR for math facts" framing.
- **Cliché kids app**: no Comic Sans or any joke-display font, no primary-rainbow palette (pure red/yellow/blue/green grid), no clip-art mascots, no cartoony characters bolted onto every screen, no balloon-pop celebration overlays.
- **Gamified-to-death**: no XP bar at the top of every screen, no slot-machine reward FX, no coin/gem currency, no daily-streak guilt-tripping, no loot-box framing. Streaks and scores exist, but they describe performance — they don't manipulate behavior.
- **Sterile ed-tech**: no Google Classroom beige, no muted institutional grays and blues, no stock-illustration "diverse students smiling at laptops," no school-portal navigation patterns.

The negative space between these four is where this app lives.

## Design Principles

1. **Latency is the headline feature.** Every interaction — submitting an answer, advancing to the next problem, switching difficulty — should feel instant. If a delay exists, it must be deliberate and short. Animations never block input.

2. **Respect the kid.** Treat the user as competent. No condescending copy, no fake praise, no over-explanation. If they get one wrong, show the right answer plainly and move on. Tone is closer to a good coach than a cheerleader.

3. **Honest reward.** Score, streak, and speed bonuses reflect real difficulty. No inflation, no participation trophies, no fake-difficulty padding. The kid should be able to tell when they actually got faster.

4. **One screen, one job.** Each route does one thing without competing for attention. The game screen shows: the problem, the input, the running session stats. Nothing else. Stats screen is a separate destination.

5. **Commit to the aesthetic.** A bold register means bold choices: strong color, confident typography, real motion. Half-measures read as timid. If a decision feels safe, push it one step further before shipping.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG AA. Standard expectations:

- Sufficient contrast for all text and meaningful UI (4.5:1 body, 3:1 large).
- Visible focus states on every interactive element; full keyboard play (Enter submits, Tab to navigate).
- Number input works with on-screen keyboard on touch devices.
- Don't rely on color alone for correct/incorrect feedback — pair color with shape, icon, or copy.

No specific user-need requirements beyond standard AA at this time. Reduced-motion support is a nice-to-have but not a hard requirement.
